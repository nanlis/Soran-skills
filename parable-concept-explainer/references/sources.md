# High-Quality Source Guide

This skill should lean on sources that help the model tell a vivid story without drifting away from the formal concept.

## Source priority

1. **Primary or official sources**
   - Seminal papers
   - Prize lectures
   - Official institutional reports
   - University-hosted course notes
2. **Authoritative secondary sources**
   - Lecture notes from well-known universities
   - Survey articles from respected journals or repositories
   - Encyclopedia entries from reputable institutions
3. **Fallback sources**
   - High-quality explainers from recognized outlets
   - Only when better sources are unavailable

## What to avoid

- SEO blogs with no author credentials
- Random summaries that do not cite original work
- Sources that only repeat the definition without showing the mechanism

## How to use sources in the final answer

- Use the sources to verify the meaning, edge cases, and standard terminology.
- Cite the sources in the final answer with links.
- If the story is inspired by a classic example from the literature, say so briefly.
- Keep the citations outside the story so the narrative stays clean.

## Example source pack for mechanism design / incentive compatibility

If the field is economics, game theory, or mechanism design, these are good source types to prefer:

- Nobel Prize lecture on mechanism design
  - Roger B. Myerson, *Perspectives on Mechanism Design in Economic Theory*
  - https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2007/myerson/lecture/
- Nobel Prize popular information on mechanism design and incentive compatibility
  - https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2007/popular-information/
- Stanford lecture notes on mechanism design basics
  - https://theory.stanford.edu/~tim/notes.html
- Official or university-hosted lecture PDF on mechanism design
  - Example PDF from NobelPrize.org: https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2018/06/maskin_lecture.pdf

## Example source pack for probability / inference

If the field is statistics or machine learning, prefer:

- University lecture notes on Bayesian inference, posterior updates, or probabilistic graphical models
- Seminal papers from arXiv, NeurIPS, ICML, JMLR, or similar venues
- Official documentation or tutorials from research labs when the concept is applied in practice

## Example source pack for law / policy / medicine

Prefer official regulations, guidelines, clinical recommendations, or court opinions. Use an explanatory source only after checking the primary rule or guideline.
