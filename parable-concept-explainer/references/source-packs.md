# Source Packs by Field

Use the pack that best matches the user’s field. The goal is to get at least one primary or near-primary source plus one strong explanatory source.

## Economics and game theory

Good for concepts that involve incentives, equilibrium, or strategy.

- [Nobel Prize mechanism design theory overview](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2007/9276-mechanism-design-theory/)
  - Use for the revelation principle, incentive compatibility, and why direct mechanisms matter.
- [Roger B. Myerson Nobel lecture](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2007/myerson/lecture/)
  - Use for the theory-level framing and canonical language.
- [Stanford Tim Roughgarden lecture notes index](https://theory.stanford.edu/~tim/notes.html)
  - Use for lecture-style explanations and approachable mathematical framing.
- [Stanford CS364B mechanism design lecture](https://theory.stanford.edu/~tim/w14/l/l32.pdf)
  - Use for Bayesian incentive compatibility and revelation principle details.
- [Cambridge chapter on incentive compatibility](https://resolve.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/A055FCB97FD608E0E66507944890E300/9780511835216c4_p38-78_CBO.pdf/incentive_compatibility.pdf)
  - Use for a textbook-style definition when available.

## Statistics and machine learning

Good for concepts that involve estimation, generalization, uncertainty, or tradeoffs.

- [Stanford CS109 inference notes](https://web.stanford.edu/class/cs109/lectures/11-Inference/)
  - Use for Bayes’ rule, posterior intuition, and introductory inference framing.
- [Stanford lecture notes index](https://web.stanford.edu/~rjohari/teaching/notes.html)
  - Use for Bayesian inference and decision-making in a course-note style.
- [Stanford MS&E 226 lecture notes](https://web.stanford.edu/class/msande226/l_notes.html)
  - Use for bias-variance tradeoffs and train/validate/test structure.
- [Berkeley regularization lecture notes](https://stat153.berkeley.edu/spring-2026/regularization-notes/)
  - Use for overfitting, cross-validation, and regularization explanations.
- [Berkeley technical report on generalization and overfitting](https://www2.eecs.berkeley.edu/Pubs/TechRpts/2019/EECS-2019-102.html)
  - Use for a more research-oriented treatment of generalization.

## Causal inference

Good for concepts with confounding, identification, treatment effects, or counterfactual reasoning.

- [Harvard causal inference lecture notes](https://imai.fas.harvard.edu/teaching/files/classic.pdf)
  - Use for canonical causal identification language.
- [Harvard research designs for causal inference](https://gov2001.hsites.harvard.edu/book/text-analysis)
  - Use for design-based explanation and methodological structure.
- [Harvard Gary King talk on simplifying causal inference](https://gking.harvard.edu/presentations/simplifying-causal-inference)
  - Use for a high-level but still serious explanation.
- [Harvard notes on observational causal inference](https://imai.fas.harvard.edu/teaching/files/obs.pdf)
  - Use for confounding, selection, and observational-data limitations.

## What each pack should contribute

For every final answer, try to assemble:

- 1 source that gives the formal definition
- 1 source that gives the mechanism or theorem
- 1 source that gives an example
- 1 source that gives a limitation or caveat

If the user’s field is outside these packs, search for the closest equivalent using the same pattern.
